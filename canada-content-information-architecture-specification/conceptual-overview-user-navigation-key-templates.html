How people find tasks: basic navigation model

The user navigation model (see Figure 2) describes how people navigate Canada.ca.

It accounts for 2 broad audiences:

  • people who may not know which institution offers the information thy are lookong for: for these people, the theme-based presentation of services and information make it easier to find what they are looking for.
  • people who do know which institution offers the information they are looking for: presenting services and information by institution meets their expectations.

As a result, the site helps people navigate in 3 ways:

  • Search: supports findability through Canada.ca search
  • Theme navigation: people can find content through theme and topic pages
  • Institutional navigation: people can find content through the departments and agencies landing page and institutional and organizational profiles

Research tells us that most Canadians looking for services and information have difficulty understanding how the government is organized. To address this, the theme-based navigation is the the priority navigation path on Canada.ca. The structure of themes and their corresponding topics is detailed in the Canada.ca Topic tree.

Content types

Institutional content helps people complete a specific task.

There are 4 user-needs categories for institutional content:

  1. Services and information
  2. Program and policy development
  3. News and promotions
  4. Corporate information

In addition, all institutional content can be identified as a specific type. See list of Canada.ca content types.

A content type typically correlates to a single category of user-needs, however certain content types may be related to multiple user-needs categories.

User-needs categories

Services and information

Services and information form the core content for which a person completes a task. Tasks are actionable information and services that deliver results for Canadians.

Access to this content must be presented under the Services and Information section of the Topic and Institutional Profile page templates.

Program and policy development

Program and policy development content provides a view into the work that the government is doing to support programs, policies and services.

This content must be presented in the “What we are doing” section of the Topic and Institutional Profile page templates.

News and promotion

This content must be presented in the “Latest news” section of the Institutional Profile Page Template, and on institutional promotional banners.

Corporate information

Corporate information is a view into the institution and its business lines, such as organizational information, proactive disclosure, and mandatory performance reporting.

Content types

A content type is a unique element describing the nature of the page content.

The tagging of content type information on Canada.ca enables:

  • the design, development and management of Canada.ca collections (see the Canada.ca collections section)
  • filtering of large lists of content based on content type (see the section on the faceted finder page)

The Canada.ca content type list includes definitions for each content type.

Content states

Content published on Canada.ca can be classified as one of 3 states. It’s classified by its use and how it’s found through navigation and search. The classifications are:

  1. current content
  2. archived content
  3. legacy content

Current content

Current content reflects Government of Canada (GC) information and services that are actively sought out by people, or that must be available to the public at all times. It conforms to content principles by being:

  • integrated
  • easy to scan
  • targeted
  • authoritative (content that is accurate and reliable)

Archived content

Archived content is information that is no longer current and is not retained within Canada.ca. Archived content is still accessible through Canada.ca for reference or to provide context for current versions of content.

Archived content must reference any current version of the content on Canada.ca through links, when current content is available. Likewise, archived content must be findable through links from the current version to support key tasks.

Archived content is indexed and can be found through the search function. This supports findability when there is no current content.

Legacy content

Legacy content is information that has been revised or superseded by more recent content, and no longer supports people’s or government needs. This content is to be removed from the website and moved into the corporate repository for recordkeeping purposes.

Information architecture

The information architecture model (see Figure 3) describes how content should be stored and accessed by publishers across Canada.ca and under institutional or organizational profiles.

Design considerations

Consistent structure and categories enable publishers to:

  • establish and maintain an effective user navigation model to ensure in-demand services and information are always easy to find
  • separate and distinguish high-demand services and information from corporate or promotional content
  • improve the ability to segment analytics by user-need categories.

Major categories

The Government of Canada (GC) has 2 major categories: theme and institution.

Theme categories

  • GC-wide topics, which only contain content following Topic Page Template guidance that:
    • provides all topic pages
  • GC-wide campaigns, which only contain content following Campaign Page Template guidance that:
    • provides information about government-wide initiative supported by more than one institution
    • provides, primarily, links to information found in either “Services and information” and “Program and policy development”
    • includes information that supports advertising campaigns, events, announcements, etc.

Institution categories

  • Corporate information consists of content such as:
    • information about an institution and its business lines
    • content generally not relatable to a Canada.ca topic, but displayed under “Corporate information” on the Institutional Profile
    • organizational charts, mandate, ministers, management, performance reporting, etc.
  • Services and information consists of content such as:
    • actionable information and services for Canadians which allow inbound communication, reporting and outbound services
    • access to service indexes which point to pages following Service Initiation Page Template guidance
    • transactional or information tasks on the Web, or the referral to another service channel (for example, phone or in-person)
    • reports, guides, digital services, databases, etc.
  • Program and policy development consists of content that:
    • provides information about programs and policies and activities related to their development
    • provides access to pages following Program Description Page Template guidance
    • can provide contextual and background information about the information and services presented on a topic page
    • includes program descriptions, policies, research papers, consultations, statistics, legislation, etc.
  • Campaigns and promotions consists of content that:
    • supports advertising campaigns, events, announcements, etc.
    • links, primarily to information found in either "Services and information" or "Program and policy development"
  • News, which only contains content following News guidance that:
    • supports media and public awareness of government activities
    • includes backgrounders, media advisories, news releases, speeches, statements, campaigns, etc.
    • includes additional media or news products not supported by GC News

Basic URL model

The Canada.ca URL model aims for consistent URL patterns throughout a person’s journey across government services, to:

  • ensure URLs are easy to understand, type and communicate
  • improve search engine optimization
  • support the consistent use of URLs in communications products
  • help URL redirections work for people and in search results

Formatting for all web pages

Each web page has a primary URL which is displayed in the browser’s address bar. All other URLs referring to the web page resolve to the primary URL, including URL aliases, redirected URLs or any other non-primary URL.

Naming convention for URLs

The following naming convention applies to all Canada.ca URLs, including those on sub-domains:

  • URLs are composed of plain language keywords and ordered from most to least relevant to minimize the risk of most relevant keywords being truncated when URLs are displayed in search results
  • avoid superfluous words such as “to”, “the”, “a”, “an”, “by”, “for” (for example, “/apply-student-loan” instead of “/how-to-apply-for-a-canadian-student-loan”
  • keywords and path segments must be separated by hyphens
  • URL must be in lowercase
  • must display unilingual keywords and in the language of the page
  • use only US-ASCII (7-bit version of ASCII) characters (for example, “meteo” instead of “météo”)
  • avoid duplicate keywords and apostrophes
  • avoid acronyms and abbreviations, unless they are better understood than the full version (for example, “RADAR” instead of “Radio Detection and Ranging”) or performs better on search engines

URLs used in communications products

URLs used in communication products are either the primary URL or a vanity URL which points to a primary URL. The following requirements and exceptions apply to URL aliases and vanity URLs:

  • easy to type, to communicate, and must follow the naming convention
  • only includes one (1) path segment (only a single slash). The file extension is hidden for HTML-based pages (for example, “canada.ca/passport”, “canada.ca/weather”)
  • communication products where the URL will be communicated verbally (for example, radio and television advertisements) may include an additional URL alias without hyphens, to make the URL easier to communicate verbally. The URL alias without hyphens is only to be communicated verbally. It is not to be communicated visually or to be communicated elsewhere, including on the web, in social media or in other communication products

    Example – Television advertisement:

    • URL communicated visually: canada.ca/employment-insurance-maternity
    • URL communicated verbally: canada.ca/employmentinsurancematernity (spoken “canada dot c a slash employment insurance maternity”)

Note: new vanity URLs and change requests must be submitted to the Principal Publisher using the Request form for Canada.ca.

Permanent redirections

Permanent redirections are implemented as follows for each of the redirection scenarios:

  • only “301 redirects” are used: the web server returns the HTTP response status code 301 Moved Permanently
  • redirections target the primary URL for the web page
  • redirections must be supported for a period of 6 months, except where a longer period is needed based on analytics
  • a “404 error” is returned once a redirection is no longer supported
  • optional: include an explanation in the destination page after a person has been redirected

Redirection scenarios

Scenarios Redirect target options (options in order of relevance)
Page was moved or the URL of the page was changed a) Redirect to the new page location or the new URL of the page

a) Redirect to the newer version of page (if it exists)

Formatting for pages published through the Managed Web Service

Canada.ca URLs align with the user navigation and information architecture models for pages in the Managed Web Service. They are structured using the following format:

[domain-name]/[language]/[path][query-string][fragment-identifier]

Domain name

The domain name for the primary URL is “canada.ca” with no sub-domains (except for “www”). This is the first segment for a URL of a web page on Canada.ca.

Note: URLs must work with and without the "www" sub-domain.

Language identifier

The second segment of the primary URL that appears after the domain name is the 2-letter ISO 639-1 language code representing the language of the page. Use "en" for English content and “fr” for French content.

Exception: where the language of the page cannot be a 2-letter ISO 639-1 language code, an ISO 639-2 or ISO 639-3 language code can be used instead.

Path

The path segments define the location of where the content appears on Canada.ca and mirrors the information architecture model. They appear directly after the language identifier. The primary URL can include 1 or more path segments, separated by slashes (directory/filename).

The file extension must be hidden for HTML-based pages (/name.html → /name) if URL rewriting or other implementation options are feasible.

Path segments are defined by 3 categories:

1. Government-wide URL

  • Composed of keywords that make sense to the user and are related to the associated navigation path and/or associated page
  • Supports government-wide communications products on Canada.ca (for example, government-wide social media channels --> “canada.ca/en/social-media“)
  • Theme, institution and information architecture path segments are excluded from government-wide URLs
  • Path segments at the government-wide level are managed by the Principal Publisher at Service Canada
  • Government-wide campaigns that span over several themes
Format

canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-navigation-page]/../[keywords-destination-page]

Example

canada.ca/en/contact/feedback

2. Theme-based URLs

  • Composed of keywords to identify the theme followed by the topic page
  • Applies only to Government of Canada (GC)-wide topic pages or GC-wide campaigns
  • For GC-wide campaigns, the path segment applies and appears between the theme keywords and the associated content keywords that make sense to the user (for example: “[keywords-theme-page]/campaigns/[keywords-destination-page]”)
  • Path segments at the theme-based level are managed by the associated theme lead institutions
Format

canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-theme-page]/[keywords-topic-page]
or
canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-theme-page]/campaigns/[keywords-navigation-page]/../[keywords-destination-page]

Example

canada.ca/en/benefits/employment-insurance
or
canada.ca/en/culture/campaigns/canada-150

3. Institution-based URLs

  • composed of keywords to identify the institution, followed by the IA, then by the associated navigation path and/or associated page
  • path segments at the institution-based level are managed by the associated institution
Format

canada.ca/[language]/[institution name]/[IA name]/[keywords-navigation-page]/../[ keywords-destination-page]

Example

canada.ca/en/parole-board/corporate/organizational-structure

Query string

Used for passing data (for example, “?parameter=value” or “/parameter/value”). This URL segment is optional.

Fragment identifier

Used to target a specific place (anchor) on the page (for example, #renew). This URL segment is optional.

Formatting for content not published through the Managed Web Service

Use one of the following options for content not hosted through the Managed Web Service. Remember to use keywords that make sense to the user when considering the destination page.

Keyword implementation options

  1. www.canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-destination-page][query-string][fragment-identifier]
  2. www.canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-navigation-page]/…/[keywords-destination-page][query-string][fragment-identifier]
  3. [keywords].canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-destination-page][query-string][fragment-identifier]
  4. [keywords].canada.ca/[language]/[keywords-navigation-page]/…/[keywords-destination-page][query-string][fragment-identifier]

Example: Application for Employment Insurance benefits online arrow pointing right employment-insurance.canada.ca/benefits/apply

Note: the language identifier is optional when publishing outside of the Managed Web service.

Sub-domains

Sub-domains are composed of plain language keywords that make sense to the user. They are only used for content or services hosted outside the Managed Web Service.

  • Sub-domains may only be created with permission from the Treasury Board Secretariat
  • A 2 keyword maximum is recommended for the sub-domain
  • For digital services that use a session to manage parameters, when the language of the page changes the sub-domain can remain in the same language as when the session first started
  • At most 1 sub-domain can be used for digital publishing platforms
  • At most 2 sub-domains can be used for digital services
  • Related sub-sites and digital services must be grouped under the same sub-domain, where possible

    Examples:

    • Application for Employment Insurance benefits online arrow pointing right employment-insurance.canada.ca/benefits/apply
    • Employment Insurance Internet reporting service arrow pointing right employment-insurance.canada.ca/reporting

Note: you must submit new Canada.ca sub-domains and change requests directly to the Principal Publisher using the Request form for Canada.ca. Requests are then reviewed by the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat.

Path segments

  • The path segments can be composed of keyword(s) derived from the digital service's title, but they should make sense to the user.
  • Additional path segments after the path segment for the page are acceptable. These additional path segments are not required to be unilingual or composed of keywords.

    The following additional options are permitted for digital services where the keyword implementation options are not technically feasible (for example, URL rewriting and other implementation options are not feasible):

    1. www.canada.ca/[string]/[keywords-destination-page]
    2. www.canada.ca/[string]/[keywords-navigation-page/…/[keywords-destination-page]
    3. [keywords].canada.ca
    4. [keywords].canada.ca/[string]/[keywords-destination-page]
    5. [keywords].canada.ca/[string]/[keywords-navigation-page/…/[keywords-destination-page]
    6. [keywords].[string].canada.ca
    7. [keywords].[string].canada.ca/[keywords-destination-page]
    8. [keywords].[string].canada.ca/[keywords-navigation-page]/…/[keywords-destination-page]
    9. [string].canada.ca/[keywords-destination-page]

    Note: “string” is a non-word string with a maximum of 5 alphanumeric characters.

  • One of the path segments can include a string pattern required by the Web application framework to trigger an action (for example, “.do” in “directory/filename.do”).
