How people find tasks: theme and institutional navigation

Canada.ca serves 2 broad audiences:

  • people who may not know which institution offers the information they are looking for
  • people who do know which institution offers the information they are looking for

As a result, Canada.ca helps people navigate in 3 different ways:

  • Theme navigation: people who do not know which institution offers what they are looking for can find it through theme and topic pages
  • Institutional navigation: people can find what they are looking for directly through the institutional presence of each department
  • Search: supports findability for both theme and institutional navigation through the on-site search engine

Most Canadians looking for services and information have difficulty understanding how the government is organized. To address this, the theme-based navigation is the priority navigation path on Canada.ca. You can find the structure of themes and their corresponding topics in the Canada.ca Topic tree.

User-need categories for destination content

User-need categories determine how users should be able to find destination content.

There are 5 user-needs categories:

  1. Services and information
  2. Program and policy development
  3. Corporate information
  4. Campaigns and promotions
  5. News

Services and information

Services and information form the core content that allows a person to complete a task. Tasks are actionable information and services that deliver results for Canadians.

Tasks can range from very simple to very complex. They can be informational or transactional. Tasks can be things like:

  • find a contact
  • get the weather forecast
  • find out what they need to report on their tax return
  • apply for a visa
  • get EI benefits
  • get ideas on what to visit in Canada
  • anything people want to do with our content

This user-need category consists of content that:

  • allows people to initiate a transaction with the government
  • answers questions people have about government services
  • focuses on what people can do or are looking for, not on what the government offers
  • helps people complete a task, and may includereports, guides, digital services or databases
  • User navigation

    This content must be accessible from both:

    • the topic tree
    • the "Services and information" section of the Institutional profile

    All your user navigation decisions should make it easier for people to find your top tasks.

  • Breadcrumb trail

    The breadcrumb trail for services and information leads back to the topic tree.

Program and policy development

Program and policy development content show what the government is doing to support programs, policies and services.

It consists of content that:

  • provides information about programs and policies and activities related to their development
  • provides background information about the information and services presented on a topic page
  • includes program descriptions, policies, research papers, consultations, statistics, legislation, etc.
  • User navigation

    This content must be accessible from the “What we are doing” section of the Topic and Institutional/Organizational profile pages, and from the "Corporate information" section of the Institutional/Orgnanizational profile pages.

  • Breadcrumb trail

    The breadcrumb trail for program and policy development leads back to institutional profile.

Corporate information

Corporate information is information about the institution itself and its business lines.

It consists of content such as:

  • institutional mandate and organizational structure
  • performance reporting and transparency
  • job opportunities
  • User navigation

    This content must be accessible from the “Corporate information” section of the Institutional Profile pages.

  • Breadcrumb trail

    The breadcrumb trail for corporate information leads back to the Institutional profile.

Campaigns and initiatives

Consists of content that:

  • supports advertising campaigns, events, announcements, etc.
  • leads people to core content found in either "Services and information" or "Program and policy development"
  • User navigation

    This content can be accessible from:

    • the “Latest news” section of the Institutional Profile
    • the Features on Institutional profiles and Topic pages
    • contextual promotions on relevant content pages
  • Breadcrumb trail

    Campaign iniatives don't need a breadcrumb trail. If you add one, it can lead back to the topic tree, the Institutional profile, or to the Home page of Canada.ca.

News

Consists of content that:

  • supports media and public awareness of government activities
  • includes backgrounders, media advisories, news releases, speeches, statements, campaigns, etc.
  • includes other media or news products not supported by GC News
  • User navigation

    This content must be accessible from the “Latest news” section of the Institutional Profile Page and in the Canada.ca newsroom.

  • Breadcrumb trail

    The breadcrumb trail for news leads back to the Institutional profile.

Basic URL model

Each web page has a primary URL, displayed in the browser’s address bar. All other URLs referring to the web page resolve to the primary URL. This applies to URL aliases, vanity URLs, redirected URLs or any other non-primary URL.

How to write URLs

This applies to all Canada.ca URLs, including vanity URLs and subdomains:

  • use plain language keywords, ordered from most to least relevant
  • avoid superfluous words such as “to”, “the”, “a”, “an”, “by”, “for” (for example, “/apply-student-loan” instead of “/how-to-apply-for-a-canadian-student-loan”
  • separate keywords and path segments by hyphens
  • use lowercase
  • it is strongly recommended to use unilingual keywords, in the language of the page
  • use only US-ASCII (7-bit version of ASCII) characters (for example, “meteo” instead of “météo”)
  • avoid duplicate keywords and apostrophes
  • avoid acronyms and abbreviations, unless they are better understood than the full version or perform better on search engines

Writing URLs for communications products

When writing URLs for communication products, you can use either the primary URL or a vanity URL.

Vanity URLs must:

  • be easy to type and to communicate
  • only include 1 path segment (only a single slash)
  • hide the file extension for HTML-based pages (for example, “canada.ca/passport”, “canada.ca/weather”)

If the URL will be spoken out loud (for example, in radio and television advertisements), you could also have another vanity URL without hyphens. This is will make it easier to read out loud.

Example – Television advertisement:

  • vanity URL for print: canada.ca/employment-insurance-maternity
  • vanity URL for television or radio: canada.ca/employmentinsurancematernity (spoken “canada dot c a slash employment insurance maternity”)

Note: submit new vanity URLs and change requests to the Principal Publisher using the Request form for Canada.ca.

Sub-domains

Only use subdomains for content or services hosted outside the Managed Web Service. Sub-domains may only be created with permission from the Treasury Board Secretariat.

When choosing as subdomain:

  • use plain language keywords that make sense to the user
  • a maximum of 2 keywords is recommended
  • it is strongly recommended to use unilingual keywords, in the language of the page
  • related sub-sites and digital services must be grouped under the same sub-domain, where possible

    Examples:

    • Application for Employment Insurance benefits online arrow pointing right employment-insurance.canada.ca/benefits/apply
    • Employment Insurance Internet reporting service arrow pointing right employment-insurance.canada.ca/reporting

Note: submit new Canada.ca sub-domains and change requests to the Principal Publisher using the Request form for Canada.ca. Requests are then reviewed by the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat.

Content types and content states

Content types

A content type is a unique element describing the nature of the page content.

The tagging of content type information on Canada.ca enables:

  • the design, development and management of Canada.ca collections (see the Canada.ca collections section)
  • filtering of large lists of content based on content type (see the section on the faceted finder page)

The Canada.ca content type list includes definitions for each content type.

Content states

There are 3 content states for content published on Canada.ca:

  1. current content
  2. archived content
  3. legacy content

Current content

Current content is information and services that are actively sought out by people. It can also be content that must be available to the public at all times.

Archived content

Archived content is information that is no longer current. It is still accessible through Canada.ca for reference or to provide context for current versions of content.

On archived content, provide links to current version of the content. If a task would require people to find archived content, provide links to the archived content on the current content.

Archived content is indexed and is findable through the search function. This supports findability when there is no current content.

Legacy content

Legacy content is information that has been revised or replaced by more recent content, and no longer supports people’s or government needs. Remove this content from the website and move it into your corporate repository for recordkeeping purposes.
